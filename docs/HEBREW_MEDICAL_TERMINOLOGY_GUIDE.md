## Professional Translation Notes

### Formality

Hebrew has formal and informal registers. For a professional medical application:

- **Use formal Hebrew** (e.g., "אתה" vs. informal "את")
- **Avoid slang** or casual expressions
- **Use professional medical terminology** from Israeli healthcare standards

### Gender Agreement

Hebrew nouns and adjectives have gender. Consider:

- **Use gender-neutral language** where possible
- **Default to masculine** when gender is unknown (standard in Hebrew)
- **Provide both forms** in user-facing text when appropriate

Example:
- Client: לקוח (m) / לקוחה (f)
- Patient: מטופל (m) / מטופלת (f)

### Right-to-Left Considerations

- **Punctuation:** Hebrew uses the same punctuation marks, but they appear on the left side in RTL text
- **Numbers:** Numbers in Hebrew text are written left-to-right (e.g., "5 בינואר" not "ינואר 5")
- **Acronyms:** Some English acronyms are used as-is (e.g., SMS, PDF, SOAP)

### Professional Review Recommended

**Critical:** Before production deployment, have translations reviewed by:
1. **Licensed Israeli therapist** (physiotherapist, massage therapist, or psychotherapist)
2. **Professional medical translator** with Hebrew expertise
3. **Native Hebrew speaker** for UI/UX terminology
